Abstraction and Introduction

While in route to the United States in April of 1912, the Titanic struck an iceberg causing passengers to flea towards a limited amount of lifeboats. The ship ultimately submerged under water less than three hours after the iceberg struck the ship. An estimated 2,224 people were on board the Titanic with over 1,500 people who perished. The purpose of this project is to analyze and explore the pattern of death and how it was distributed among class, gender, and age aboard the Titanic. The data that will be used contains passenger information while outlining who survived. As relationships between certain variables and death rates are established, visualizations will be produced to illustrate them. The problem statement will be addressed using techniques and tools that can be found in the R language and its library packages.

Required Packages

The majority of work can be done using Base-R. Other libraries will be added for data manipulation and visualization. Data manipulation is needed for data exploration and will include the dplyr and tidyverse libraries. Data visualization is needed to gain insights from the data that would not otherwise have been clear using other tools. These libraries include ggplot2, and RColorBrewer. Ggplot2 is a powerful graphics language for creating elegant and complex plots. The idea is to provide the data and tell the function how to map variables to the aesthetics.

Exploratory Data Analysis

The ultimate goal of the exploratory data analysis process is to determine the likelihood of survival among certain groups of passengers. For example, we should be able to determine if a young woman in first class has a better chance of survival than an older man in third class. In order to achieve this, first, the characteristics (class, sex, and age) will be compared to the survival column individually. Later, they will be compared to the survival column grouped together in various ways. Ggplot will be heavily used in this section to create data visualizations.
The first column that needs to be evaluated is the survival column without any other variables. To do this, a bar chart will be created using geom_bar() function. For plotting purposed, the 0 and 1 variables in the survived column will be changed to died or survived. 

# Mutate Survived Column
titanic <- titanic %>%
  mutate(survived = ifelse(survived == 0,
                           "Died",
                           "Survived"))
# Survival Amount
ggplot(titanic,
       mapping = aes(x = survived)) +
  geom_bar(width = .95,
           position = "stack",
           fill = "#70ecc0") +
  theme_light() +
  labs(x = "",
       y = "Number of People",
       title = "Survival Amount") +
  geom_text(stat = 'count',
            aes(label =..count..,
                vjust = -0.2))

After plotting the amount of passengers who died or survived, it is evident that more people had died than survived the crash. As shown in the plot, 619 passengers died while 427 survived. This means, without contributng any other variable, each passenger had about a 40 percent chance of surviving. The first variable that will individually be compare to the survival variable will be gender. This plot is similiar to the above with an added aesthetic to fill with the gender percentage. 


# Survival Distribution Among Gender
titanic %>%
  ggplot(mapping = aes(x = survived,
                       fill = sex)) +
  geom_bar(width = .95,
           position = "stack") +
  theme_light()+
  labs(x = "",
       y = "Number of People",
       title = "Gender Distribution Among Survival",
       fill = "Gender") +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#70ecc0",
                               "#21597c")) +
  geom_text(stat = 'count',
            aes(label =..count..),
            position = position_stack(vjust = 0.5),
            size = 5)


By illustrating the relationship between the gender and survived column, it is evident that males had a much higher chance of passing away than females. Of the passengers who were male, 523 died while 123 survived. Of the passengers who were female, 96 died while 292 survived. Meaning, males had a 20.5 percent chance of survival compared to the 75.25 percent chance of survival that women had.
The relationship that will be illustrated next is survival and how it is distributed among age. The plot will be similiar to the gender plot, however, we will use the geom_density so any overlap can be seen. 

# Survival Rate Among Age
titanic %>%

By illustrating the relationship between the age and survived column, it can be shown that there are only two age group where the amount of people survived exeeds the amount of people who passed away. Those approximate age groups are between 0 to 18 and 45 to 55. All other age groups have more passengers who passed away than survived.
The next relationship that will be established is the relationship between passengers who survived and their class. This can be done using a geom bar with the pclass column on the x-axis and filled with the survived column.

Now that new categories and groups have been created, ggplot can be used to illustrate the relationship between the new category column and the survived column. Since the goal is to determine the percentage of chance of survival among each category, a new column of percentages will be created. This new column will be calculated by count / sum(count). To plot, the categories will be on the y axis, percentage on the x axis, and filled with the survived column. To illustrate this relationship geom_bar will be used. 
titanic <- titanic %>%
  group_by(category, survived) %>%
  summarise(count = n()) %>%
  mutate(perc = count / sum(count))


ggplot(titanic, aes(y = factor(category), x = perc * 100, fill = factor(survived))) +
  geom_bar(stat = "identity", width = 0.95) +
  labs(x = "Percent",
       y = "Gender, Age, Class Group",
       fill = "") +
  theme_light() +
 theme(legend.position = "top") +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#70ecc0",
